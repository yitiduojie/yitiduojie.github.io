# Multidimensional Quantum Dynamics: MCTDH Theory and Applications

Authors: Hans-Dieter Meyer, Fabien Gatti, Graham A. Worth Question is: what are the differences between quantum chemistry and quantum dyamics? One difference is that quantum chemistry is characterized by low quantum numbers.

In Quantum chem.:

  1. usually interested in the electronic ground state;
  2. molecular orbitals have a simple smooth form and can be well represented by Gaussian basis sets. (Oppenheimer approx. is usually can be used.)

In quantum dynamics, however, the wave function is much more structured and may contain highly oscillatory terms. (L: considered the highly exited states, which is much sensitive to the state of the nuclei than the electrons in ground states. ?) Moreover, the density of states is rather high. There may be hundreds of eigenstates lying below a fundamental C–H or O–H stretch of a small polyatomic molecule (four or five atoms, say). This high density of states, which is a consequence of the fact that nuclear masses are much larger than the electron mass, is one of the main sources of difficulties.

A second difference is that quantum chemistry is governed by the Coulomb potential, a rather structureless two-body interaction. Quantum dynamics, on the other hand, has to cope with complicated many-body potentials, which are not general but specific for the problem under investigation. The strong repulsion suffered by atoms that come close to each other may lead to a very strongly correlated motion. These differences explain why the techniques used in quantum chemistry and quantum dynamics are often similar in spirit but very different in detail.

The development of quantum dynamics simulations has been driven by research probing the fundamental properties of molecules. Historically, the first field of importance was scattering experiments using molecular beams. To study elementary reactions, it is necessary to enable molecules to collide with known initial states under controlled conditions and to measure the products of the collision. One way to achieve this experimentally is in scattering experiments using beams of molecules. In particular, crossed molecular beam experiments, pioneered by Lee, Herschbach, Toennies and others, have provided a wealth of data in this field.
